Triage a Drug-Safety Report with an AI Crew — and Trust What It Doesn't Know — Tutorial¶

In ~30 minutes, no code, you build a two-agent pharmacovigilance intake crew, run it on an adverse-event report you paste, and watch it extract a structured safety case, assess seriousness, and — the part that matters in a regulated domain — flag every field it isn't sure of instead of fabricating it, all in an auditable trace, deployed in your own workspace.

Step 1 — Describe what you want (Crew Studio)¶

A drug-safety team lives under a firehose of adverse-event reports — e-faxes, emails, literature. Each one has to be read, the case extracted, the seriousness assessed, and a narrative written for the safety database. It's meticulous, high-volume, and unforgiving: a fabricated or mis-coded value can harm a patient and trigger a regulatory finding. That combination — high volume and a hard accuracy bar — is exactly where a crew of AI agents helps, if it's honest about what it doesn't know. Let's build that crew, no code.

Open Crew Studio (left rail, under Build), and in Describe your automation paste this, then hit the blue →:

Process an adverse-event drug-safety report for pharmacovigilance intake. Given the pasted text of an adverse-event report, first have a case intake specialist extract a structured Individual Case Safety Report (reporter, patient, suspect drugs, adverse events and their seriousness, key dates) with an honest confidence score per field, never fabricating safety-critical values. Then have a medical safety reviewer assess seriousness and expectedness, flag missing or low-confidence critical fields, and draft a case narrative marking anything uncertain.

💡 What just happened. You described the job, in plain English, and Studio will propose a crew — a team of agents that work in sequence. Notice you asked for two things that matter in a regulated domain: confidence scoring, and never inventing safety-critical values.

✅ Checkpoint: Studio opens a project editor with a Studio Chat panel and a Canvas.

Step 2 — Steer the build (keep it self-contained)¶

Studio works out how to build the crew. It may ask you a clarifying question or two, or go straight to proposing a plan — both are normal.

If it asks, choose the self-contained options so everything runs in your browser with nothing to connect: Plain text input (manually pasted) for where the report comes from, and Display in the crew output only for the result.

If it goes straight to a plan, just read it and continue.

💡 Why self-contained matters. Real PV intake wires up e-fax, email, and safety databases — but for learning, "paste the report text, read the result on screen" means you need no integrations, no admin, nothing but this browser. (Studio is an AI; it won't ask the same thing every time — steer by that principle, not a fixed script.)

✅ Checkpoint: Studio shows a plan (it may ask a question or two first, or go straight to it).

Step 3 — Confirm the plan → your crew appears¶

Read the plan — you'll see your two agents — then click Confirm plan. (Nothing is built until you confirm.) Watch the Canvas build:

  • a Case Intake Specialist — extracts the structured safety case (ICSR), scoring confidence per field and leaving safety-critical values null rather than guessing.
  • a Medical Safety Reviewer — a second agent that assesses seriousness, flags the low-confidence gaps, and drafts the narrative (marking uncertain items). (Studio names these something like Pharmacovigilance Case Intake Specialist / Senior Medical Safety Reviewer — wording varies.)

They run sequentially: intake extracts, then the reviewer double-checks and writes it up. (A suggestion tip may appear in the chat panel — leave it open; it often holds the Run Automation button you'll use next, so dismissing it just hides that button.) Studio also picks a project name on its own — that varies and doesn't matter; what matters is the two agents wired intake → reviewer.

💡 The idea worth keeping. The reviewer is your second set of eyes — the same control a real PV process demands before a case is trusted.

✅ Checkpoint: The Canvas shows two agents wired in order — a case intake specialist → a medical safety reviewer (Studio's auto-generated project name varies).

Step 4 — Run it on an adverse-event report¶

Start a run by clicking the blue Run button (top right) — that reliably opens the run form. (You may also see a black Run Automation button inside the Studio Chat panel; it does the same thing. Don't switch to the "Run" tab expecting a button — it can just show a "waiting for events…" spinner.) You'll get a form (titled Run parameters) with a box for the report text, labeled with the raw field name (e.g. ae_report_text).

Paste a synthetic, fictional adverse-event report — use this one, or write your own with a few details deliberately missing:

Spontaneous report received 2026-07-10 from a community pharmacist in Ohio, USA. Patient: 68-year-old female. Suspect drug: Veltrimyxin 50 mg tablets, started around 2026-06-28 for hypertension, once daily. On 2026-07-08 she developed a widespread rash, facial swelling, and difficulty breathing; she went to the ED and was hospitalized overnight. Events: angioedema, dyspnea, urticaria. Outcome: recovering. Concomitant meds: "a water pill" (name not given). The pharmacist was unsure of the exact start date and didn't have the patient's weight. No prior allergy history stated.

Click Execute.

💡 The planted gaps are the point. Weight, the concomitant drug name, the exact date — all missing. Watch what the crew does with what it doesn't know.

✅ Checkpoint: The Run/execution view shows Execution started and a live Timeline.

Step 5 — Watch it think — and flag what it doesn't know¶

The Timeline shows each agent run — the intake specialist, then the safety reviewer — each making an LLM call (the AI model reasoning) you can inspect. Click the reviewer's Completed step — then scroll past the Description (the instructions) to the Output section below to read the result. On a real run of the report above it returned:

Seriousness: SERIOUS (hospitalization criterion met — angioedema, dyspnea, urticaria). Expectedness: Unexpected — marked [UNCERTAIN] when labeling isn't available. Critical Field Flags: weight — confidence 0, null → obtain; medical history — null; route — null … each with a follow-up action. Then an ICH-E2E case narrative with every non-source detail marked [UNCERTAIN], and a reportability note deferring the final call to a qualified person.

💡 Why this is the whole point. It didn't guess the weight or invent a dose — it flagged what it doesn't know and told you what to follow up. Paste a sparser report and the flags multiply: the crew gets more honest, not more confident. That's the difference between an AI you can put near a regulated safety process and one you can't — and the trace is your audit trail.

✅ Checkpoint: You've read the seriousness call, the Critical Field Flags, and the [UNCERTAIN]-marked narrative in the trace.

Step 6 — Deploy it as a real service¶

Make it real: click Deploy (top right). In Ready to go live, keep the suggested name (whatever Studio generated), optionally add a release note, and click Deploy.

Go to Automations (left rail) — your crew is there, Source Studio. It first shows Building Images for Crew…, then goes Online, with its own endpoint (a web address other systems can call) and an access token. This can take a few minutes — if it looks stuck on Building Images for Crew, refresh the page and it'll flip to Online. Identify the one you just created (the newest entry) — it now runs without your editor open, a service your safety team (or an intake pipeline) can call.

💡 What you just crossed. From a prototype in a tool to a running, auditable safety-intake service in your own workspace — every run landing in the same kind of trace you just read.

What you did: in ~30 minutes, no code — you built a two-agent pharmacovigilance intake crew, ran it on a report you chose, watched it extract a structured case and honestly flag the gaps, and deployed it. Take it further: paste a messy real-world-shaped report and watch the confidence scores move · feed it a non-serious case and see the seriousness call flip · the same shape works for literature intake, complaint handling, or medical-information requests.

✅ Checkpoint: Your newly-deployed crew (the newest Studio-source entry) is listed under Automations and goes Online.
