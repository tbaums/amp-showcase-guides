Triage a Consumer Complaint with an AI Crew — and Catch the One That's a Recall — Instructor Lab¶

Target time: 40 min · Steps: 6

In ~30 minutes, no code, you build a two-agent consumer-complaint triage crew, paste in a raw complaint, and watch it classify and extract the details, judge severity and regulatory reportability, escalate the one that's a genuine safety event — and, the part that matters when a recall might be on the line, flag the missing critical info (like the lot code) instead of inventing it, then draft an empathetic consumer reply, all in an auditable trace, deployed in your own workspace.

Step 1/6: Describe what you want (Crew Studio)¶

Step 1 — Describe what you want (Crew Studio)¶

A consumer-goods company's complaint queue is a firehose — call-center notes, emails, web-form submissions. Most are gripes about a dented box or a flavor someone didn't like. But every so often one is a foreign object in the food with an injury — a potential safety event, maybe a recall trigger — and it's sitting in the exact same queue, triaged by whoever picked up the phone. Miss it, or worse, fabricate a detail on it, and you have a regulatory problem. Classifying complaints, judging severity, and being honest about what's missing, at volume, is exactly what a crew of AI agents is good at — if you can trust it. Let's build that crew, no code.

Open Crew Studio from the left rail (under Build). You'll land on "What would you like to build today?" with a Describe your automation box. Paste this in and hit the blue → (Build automation):

Triage a consumer product complaint for a consumer-goods company. Given the pasted text of a complaint (from a call-center note, an email, or a web form), first have an intake specialist classify the complaint into a category (safety / quality / packaging / customer-service), extract the product and incident details (product name, batch or lot code, purchase channel, date, and what happened), assign a confidence level to each extracted field, and flag any potential safety issue or regulatory-reportable event, never fabricating details the text does not contain. Then have a quality-assurance reviewer assess the severity and whether it is reportable, flag any missing critical information, recommend the handling and escalation path (log and close / quality follow-up / urgent safety escalation), and draft a short empathetic response to the consumer.

💡 What just happened. You didn't pick a template — you described the job in plain English. Studio reads that and proposes a crew: a team of AI agents, each with a role, that work in sequence. That's the core CrewAI idea, and you'll watch it take shape.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — Studio opens a project editor with a Studio Chat panel and a Canvas. · Target time: ~7 min

Step 2/6: Steer the build (keep it self-contained)¶

Step 2 — Steer the build (keep it self-contained)¶

Studio now works out how to build your crew. It may do one of two things — either ask you a couple of clarifying questions first, or go straight to proposing a plan. Both are normal; on our build it went straight to the plan, with both agents already using no external tools.

If it asks questions, answer with the self-contained option each time so everything runs right here in your browser — nothing to connect: - When it asks where the complaint comes from → choose Plain text input (manually pasted) (not Zendesk, a call-center system, or an inbox). - When it asks where the output should go → choose Display in the crew output only (not a ticketing system, Sheet, or email).

If it goes straight to a plan, great — read it and confirm both agents show Tools: None (this crew reasons purely over the pasted complaint; it doesn't need to search or scrape anything).

💡 Why this matters. Those self-contained choices are the difference between a demo that needs your company's ticketing system, mail server, and admin — and one that just works for you, right now. "Paste the complaint in, read the triage on screen" is the whole no-setup story. (Studio is an AI — it won't ask the exact same thing every time, so steer by that principle rather than by a fixed script.)

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — Studio shows a plan (it may ask a question or two first, or go straight to it). · Target time: ~7 min

Step 3/6: Confirm the plan → your crew appears¶

Step 3 — Confirm the plan → your crew appears¶

Read the plan Studio proposes — you'll recognize your two agents, plus a single input variable {complaint_text} — then click Confirm plan. (Nothing is built until you confirm; if you tried to Run now you'd get "No tasks found.")

Watch the Canvas build in front of you:

  • an intake specialist — reads the raw complaint, classifies it (safety / quality / packaging / customer-service), extracts the product and incident details (name, lot/batch code, purchase channel, date, what happened) with a confidence level on each, and flags anything that looks like a safety or regulatory-reportable event. (Studio names it something like Consumer Complaint Intake Specialist — the exact wording varies.)
  • a QA & regulatory reviewer — a second agent that judges severity, decides reportability, lists the missing critical info, sets the escalation path, and drafts the consumer reply. (Named something like Quality Assurance & Regulatory Reviewer.)

They run sequentially: the specialist intakes, then the reviewer judges and escalates. (A suggestion tip may appear in the chat panel — leave it open; it often holds a Run Automation button, so dismissing it just hides that button.) Studio also picks a project name on its own (e.g. complaint-triage-crew) — that varies run to run and doesn't matter; what matters is the two agents wired intake → QA.

💡 The idea worth keeping. You didn't ask for a second reviewer — but any complaint process that touches product safety needs one: the person who logs a complaint isn't the one who decides it's FDA-reportable. Notice AMP gave your crew that same separation — a QA agent that reviews the intake and makes the reportability call before anything is closed. That's why you can trust the escalation later.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — The Canvas shows two agents wired in order — an intake specialist → a QA & regulatory reviewer (Studio's auto-generated project name varies). · Target time: ~7 min

Step 4/6: Run it on a consumer complaint¶

Step 4 — Run it on a consumer complaint¶

Start a run by clicking the blue Run button (top right) — that reliably opens the run form. (You may also see a Run Automation button inside the Studio Chat panel; it does the same thing. Don't switch to the "Run" tab expecting a button — it can just show a "waiting for events…" spinner.) Because you chose plain-text input, there's nothing to connect — AMP gives you a simple form (titled Run parameters) with one box per input the plan generated; each box is labeled with the raw field name (on our build there was a single field, complaint_text).

Paste in the complaint below. It's deliberately the scary one in the queue — a genuine safety event, but reported by an upset consumer who doesn't have half the details:

CONSUMER COMPLAINT — logged by a call-center agent

Consumer (gave first name only, "Renata") called about a jar of our "GleamRoast Hazelnut Spread." She says she bit into it and there was "something hard and sharp, like a piece of plastic or maybe glass," in the spread, and it "cut the inside of my mouth a little." She was shaken and upset.

She does not have the jar with her and could not read the lot or batch code — "I already threw the packaging out." She isn't sure where she bought it — "one of the big grocery chains, I think, maybe a few weeks ago?" She could not give a purchase date. She said she "might go to the doctor if it keeps hurting" but has not seen one yet. She asked whether we'd send a refund, gave a callback number, and asked whether anyone else had reported the same thing with this product.

Your form may not match this exactly. Studio designs the inputs from your description, so the field name/count can vary run to run. If you see a single complaint_text box, paste the whole complaint in. If your build split it into several fields, map the complaint across the boxes you see.

Fill every box the form shows, then click Execute.

💡 This is "your own data." Swap in a routine gripe — a dented box, a flavor someone disliked — and re-run. Watch the severity drop and the handling flip to Log and Close. The crew reasons about what you gave it, not a canned script.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — The Run/execution view shows Execution started and a live Timeline. · Target time: ~7 min

Step 5/6: Watch it think — escalate the real one, flag what's missing¶

Step 5 — Watch it think — escalate the real one, flag what's missing¶

This is the part a slide deck can't fake. The Timeline shows each agent as it runs — each one makes an LLM call (the AI model doing the actual reasoning), and you can watch it happen:

  • Consumer Complaint Intake Specialist → classify & extract: Started → LLM call (~4s) → Completed
  • QA & Regulatory Reviewer → QA Triage Report: Started → LLM call (~5s) → Completed

Click any step to open Event details (with a Raw Data tab) — the exact instruction the agent got and what it produced. Click the QA reviewer's Completed step — then scroll past the Description (the agent's instructions) to the Output section below, which is the actual final result. On a real run of the GleamRoast complaint above it returned a QA Triage Report with:

1. Severity: Critical — a hard/sharp object that caused an injury is an immediate safety risk. 2. Regulatory Reportability: Reportable — a foreign-object injury in a food product meets FDA food-safety reporting criteria. 3. Missing Critical Information — Batch / Lot Code ("crucial for tracking and recalling affected products if necessary"), Purchase Channel, Purchase/Incident Date. 4. Recommended Handling: Urgent Safety Escalation — injury + potential contamination warrants immediate investigation. 5. Consumer Response Draft — a short, empathetic, professional reply that apologizes, promises to investigate, makes no legal admission, and signs off "your case number is [CASE-XXXX]."

Look at two things there. First, it didn't treat a recall-grade complaint as a customer-service ticket — it escalated it. Second, it flagged the missing lot code and told you why it matters (you can't run a recall without it), and in the consumer reply it wrote [CASE-XXXX] rather than inventing a case number to look finished.

💡 Why this is the whole point. You can see which agent decided what, on which evidence, and how long it took — and you can see it escalate what's dangerous while admitting what it doesn't know. When your QA lead asks "why did this get escalated and what do we still need?", the answer isn't "the AI said so" — it's a trace you can open, with the reportability reasoning and the missing-info list laid out. In a domain where a fabricated lot code or a missed safety event is a regulatory finding, an AI that flags the blank instead of filling it is the one you can actually put in the queue.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — You've read the severity + reportability call, the escalation path, and seen a missing critical field flagged rather than fabricated in the trace. · Target time: ~7 min

Step 6/6: Deploy it as a real service¶

Step 6 — Deploy it as a real service¶

A crew that only runs in the editor is a prototype. Make it real: click Deploy (top right). In Ready to go live, keep the suggested name (whatever Studio generated, e.g. Complaint Triage Crew-v1), optionally add a release note, and click Deploy.

Go to Automations in the left rail — your crew is there, Source Studio. It first shows Provisioning Crew / Building Images for Crew…, then goes Online, with its own endpoint (a web address other systems can call) and an access token. This can take a few minutes — if it looks stuck on Building Images for Crew, refresh the page and it'll flip to Online. Identify the one you just created (it's the newest entry — don't confuse it with an older, similarly-named crew someone made before). It now runs without your editor open — a service you (or the ticketing system you connect later) can call.

💡 What you just crossed. From "I built something in a tool" to "I have a running service in my workspace." Same crew, now deployed — and every complaint you run against it shows up in the same kind of trace you just read.

What you did: in ~30 minutes, no code — you described a complaint-triage crew, built it (an intake specialist + a QA & regulatory reviewer), ran it on the scary complaint in the queue, read the trace to see exactly how it judged severity and reportability — including watching it escalate the real safety event and flag the missing lot code instead of inventing it — and deployed it as a live service in your own workspace. Every claim it made, you could check.

Take it further: feed it a routine gripe and watch it log-and-close · paste a complaint with the packaging already discarded and watch the missing-info flags multiply · deploy a second version and compare · swap the scenario for your own domain (the same shape works for claims triage, fraud triage, or pharmacovigilance intake).

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — Your newly-deployed crew (the newest Studio-source entry) is listed under Automations and goes Online. · Target time: ~7 min
