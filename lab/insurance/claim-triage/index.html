Triage an Insurance Claim with an AI Crew — and Watch It Refuse to Guess — Instructor Lab¶

Target time: 40 min · Steps: 6

In ~30 minutes, no code, you build a two-agent first-notice-of-loss (FNOL) triage crew, paste in a raw claim report, and watch it extract a structured claim with per-field confidence, flag every missing field and fraud indicator, recommend a routing decision — and, the part that matters when money and regulators are involved, leave the fields it can't support blank instead of inventing them, all in an auditable trace, deployed in your own workspace.

Step 1/6: Describe what you want (Crew Studio)¶

Step 1 — Describe what you want (Crew Studio)¶

A claims intake team's day is a firehose of first-notice-of-loss (FNOL) reports — phone calls typed up by an agent, e-faxes, portal submissions. Each one has to be read, structured, checked for coverage and fraud signals, and routed to the right queue. Get a value wrong and you either pay a fraudster or deny a real customer — and you have to defend the decision later. That judgment work, at volume, is exactly what a crew of AI agents is good at — if you can trust it. Let's build that crew, no code.

Open Crew Studio from the left rail (under Build). You'll land on "What would you like to build today?" with a Describe your automation box. Paste this in and hit the blue → (Build automation):

Triage a first-notice-of-loss insurance claim. Given the pasted text of a claim or loss report, first have a claims intake analyst extract a structured claim (policyholder name, policy reference, incident type and date, item or loss description, and claimed amount), assigning a confidence score to each field, flagging any missing information and any fraud indicators, and never fabricating values. Then have a claims adjuster reviewer assess coverage applicability and claim severity, flag the low-confidence or missing fields and the fraud risk, recommend routing (fast-track settlement / standard adjuster review / SIU fraud investigation), and draft a concise claim summary note for the adjuster.

💡 What just happened. You didn't pick a template — you described the job in plain English. Studio reads that and proposes a crew: a team of AI agents, each with a role, that work in sequence. That's the core CrewAI idea, and you'll watch it take shape.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — Studio opens a project editor with a Studio Chat panel and a Canvas. · Target time: ~7 min

Step 2/6: Steer the build (keep it self-contained)¶

Step 2 — Steer the build (keep it self-contained)¶

Studio now works out how to build your crew. It may do one of two things — either ask you a couple of clarifying questions first, or go straight to proposing a plan. Both are normal; on our build it went straight to the plan.

If it asks questions, answer with the self-contained option each time so everything runs right here in your browser — nothing to connect: - When it asks where the claim report comes from → choose Plain text input (manually pasted) (not Gmail/a claims portal/an inbox). - When it asks where the output should go → choose Display in the crew output only (not a Sheet, email, or ticket system).

If it goes straight to a plan, great — just read it and move to the next step.

💡 Why this matters. Those self-contained choices are the difference between a demo that needs your carrier's claims portal, mail server, and admin — and one that just works for you, right now. "Paste the report in, read the triage on screen" is the whole no-setup story. (Studio is an AI — it won't ask the exact same thing every time, so steer by that principle rather than by a fixed script.)

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — Studio shows a plan (it may ask a question or two first, or go straight to it). · Target time: ~7 min

Step 3/6: Confirm the plan → your crew appears¶

Step 3 — Confirm the plan → your crew appears¶

Read the plan Studio proposes — you'll recognize your two agents in it, plus a single input variable {claim_text} — then click Confirm plan. (Nothing is built until you confirm; if you tried to Run now you'd get "No tasks found.")

Watch the Canvas build in front of you:

  • an intake analyst — reads the raw report and extracts a structured claim (policyholder, policy reference, incident type & date, loss description, claimed amount), scoring each field's confidence and flagging what's missing or looks like a fraud indicator. (Studio names it something like Insurance Claims Intake Analyst — the exact wording varies.)
  • a claims adjuster reviewer — a second agent that takes the analyst's structured claim, assesses coverage and severity, consolidates the risks, recommends a routing decision, and drafts the adjuster note. (Named something like Senior Claims Adjuster & Coverage Analyst.)

They run sequentially: the analyst extracts, then the adjuster reviews and routes. (A suggestion tip may appear in the chat panel — leave it open; it often holds a Run Automation button, so dismissing it just hides that button.) Studio also picks a project name on its own (e.g. FNOL Claim Triage Crew) — that varies run to run and doesn't matter; what matters is the two agents wired analyst → adjuster.

💡 The idea worth keeping. You didn't ask for a checker — but good claims process always has one: the person who intakes a claim isn't the person who approves the payout. Notice AMP gave your crew that same separation — an adjuster agent that reviews the intake before anything is routed. That second set of eyes is why you can trust the output later.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — The Canvas shows two agents wired in order — a claims intake analyst → a claims adjuster reviewer (Studio's auto-generated project name varies). · Target time: ~7 min

Step 4/6: Run it on a claim report¶

Step 4 — Run it on a claim report¶

Start a run by clicking the blue Run button (top right) — that reliably opens the run form. (You may also see a Run Automation button inside the Studio Chat panel; it does the same thing. Don't switch to the "Run" tab expecting a button — it can just show a "waiting for events…" spinner.) Because you chose plain-text input, there's nothing to connect — AMP gives you a simple form (titled Run parameters) with one box per input the plan generated; each box is labeled with the raw field name (on our build there was a single field, claim_text).

Paste in the FNOL report below. It's a deliberately messy claim — designed with real red flags and real gaps so you can watch the crew handle both:

FIRST NOTICE OF LOSS — phone intake, typed up by the call-center agent

Caller gave his name as "Marlon Fissbeck." Says his cellphone was stolen. States he has a policy with us but could not give the policy number — said it "starts with a P, I think." Could not confirm the address on file.

On the loss: says it was "the expensive top-of-the-line model, the newest one." When was it taken? He wasn't sure — "a few weeks back, maybe around the holidays." Says he only noticed it missing recently and is calling it in now.

He wants to claim $3,000.00 — said "just make it three grand even, that's about what it's worth, round it up." Also stated the phone was "brand new, still had the plastic wrap on it," but later in the same call mentioned he'd "been using it for months for work."

No police report was filed. Says the theft happened "at a big crowded event" but could not name the venue or the city. Our intake notes indicate this policy was opened 9 days ago. Caller repeatedly asked how quickly he could be paid out and whether it could be same-day.

Your form may not match this exactly. Studio designs the inputs from your description, so the field name/count can vary run to run. If you see a single claim_text box, paste the whole report in. If your build split it into several fields, just map the report across the boxes you see.

Fill every box the form shows, then click Execute.

💡 This is "your own data." Swap in a clean, complete claim — real policy number, a specific date, an itemized receipt amount — and re-run. Watch the routing flip toward fast-track. The crew reasons about what you gave it, not a canned script.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — The Run/execution view shows Execution started and a live Timeline. · Target time: ~7 min

Step 5/6: Watch it think — and refuse to guess¶

Step 5 — Watch it think — and refuse to guess¶

This is the part a slide deck can't fake. The Timeline shows each agent as it runs — each one makes an LLM call (the AI model doing the actual reasoning), and you can watch it happen:

  • Claims Intake Analyst → Extract Structured Claim: Started → LLM call (~6s) → Completed
  • Claims Adjuster Reviewer → Review and Route Claim: Started → LLM call (~8s) → Completed

Click any step to open Event details (with a Raw Data tab) — the exact instruction the agent got and what it produced. Click the adjuster's Completed step — then scroll past the Description (the agent's instructions) to the Output section below, which is the actual final result. On a real run of the messy claim above it returned an Adjuster Review Document with:

Severity: Moderate. Risk & Flag Summary — it caught all of it: (1) missing policy reference, (2) missing incident date, (3) the round-number $3,000 ("raises potential fraud flags"), (4) the contradiction about the phone's condition (brand-new vs. used for months), (5) no police report, (6) delayed reporting. Routing: 📋 Standard Adjuster Review — and it justified the call: "while there are potential fraud indicators, they do not yet provide conclusive evidence to warrant a SIU investigation."

Then look at the Adjuster Summary Note — the last section. This is the moment worth pausing on:

"On [insert incident date once confirmed], the policyholder Marlon Fissbeck reported a theft of a mobile phone claimed valued at $3,000…"

It didn't invent a date. The report never gave one, so the crew left a placeholder and told the adjuster to confirm it — instead of quietly fabricating a plausible-looking date to fill the blank.

💡 Why this is the whole point. You can see which agent decided what, on which evidence, and how long it took — and you can see it flag what it doesn't know rather than guess. When your claims lead asks "why did it route this to review, not SIU?" the answer isn't "the AI said so" — it's a trace you can open, with the six risk signals and the reasoning laid out. In a domain where a made-up value pays a fraudster or denies a real customer, an AI that leaves the blank blank is the one you can actually put near a payout.

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — You've read the routing recommendation, the Risk & Flag Summary, and seen a missing field left blank/flagged rather than fabricated in the trace. · Target time: ~7 min

Step 6/6: Deploy it as a real service¶

Step 6 — Deploy it as a real service¶

A crew that only runs in the editor is a prototype. Make it real: click Deploy (top right). In Ready to go live, keep the suggested name (whatever Studio generated, e.g. FNOL Claim Triage Crew-v1), optionally add a release note, and click Deploy.

Go to Automations in the left rail — your crew is there, Source Studio. It first shows Deploy Enqueued / Building Images for Crew…, then goes Online, with its own endpoint (a web address other systems can call) and an access token. This can take a few minutes — if it looks stuck on Building Images for Crew, refresh the page and it'll flip to Online. Identify the one you just created (it's the newest entry — don't confuse it with an older, similarly-named crew someone made before). It now runs without your editor open — a service you (or a claims system you connect later) can call.

💡 What you just crossed. From "I built something in a tool" to "I have a running service in my workspace." Same crew, now deployed — and every claim you run against it shows up in the same kind of trace you just read.

What you did: in ~30 minutes, no code — you described an FNOL-triage crew, built it (an intake analyst + an adjuster reviewer), ran it on a messy claim you chose, read the trace to see exactly how it decided — including watching it flag what it couldn't verify instead of inventing it — and deployed it as a live service in your own workspace. Every claim it made, you could check.

Take it further: feed it a clean, complete claim and watch the routing flip to fast-track · paste a sparse report and watch the missing-field flags multiply · deploy a second version and compare · swap the scenario for your own domain (the same shape works for fraud triage, pharmacovigilance intake, or consumer-complaint triage).

Instructor note: confirm the room hits this checkpoint before moving on — Your newly-deployed crew (the newest Studio-source entry) is listed under Automations and goes Online. · Target time: ~7 min
